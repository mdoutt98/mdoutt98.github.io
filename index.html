Chris and Matt straggled into town a while later, and together we had dinner at the local Mexican restaurant, which kindly stayed open late to provide us with Mexican cokes (made with sugar instead of corn syrup) and burritos made with a motley assortment of leftovers. After a fun evening of eating and catching up, we retired to our motel room, excited for the small town July 4th festivities that awaited us in the morning.

We rolled out of bed relaxed and ready for our day off. We made our way to main street for the town parade, which seemed to have drawn most of the town population of 1400. Firetrucks and ambulances rode past with volunteers throwing candy to excited little kids and Matt, while a notable number of townspeople of all ages rode by in rodeo gear on horses. After taking in the parade and some lunch from Subway, we headed to the town pool, which was free for the afternoon. The western Utah sun was scorching, so the cool pool was a welcome relief, even if it was swarming with kids, making us feel a bit out of place. An eclectic mix of children and adults was taking turns doing some impressive flips and dives from the diving board, so we watched the action while relaxing in a less-crowded corner of the pool. After whiling away some of the afternoon, we stopped by the playground to do some pull-ups for a bit of upper body exercise. We wound up spending more time playing on the monkey bars and a merry-go-round like contraption, which led to a lot of fun and Peter tearing a decent chunk of skin off of his hands. With the playground hijinks finished, we returned to the pool, where we met Chris, Matt, and even old friend Mercedes, who had also caught up to us. After yet more hanging out, we retired to our motel room to recharge after a long day of relaxation. As evening fell, we made our way to the high school track to watch the fireworks. There we met Chris and Matt, and, together with the majority of Milford, we celebrated our nation’s independence and enjoyed a relatively impressive fireworks display.

Our wonderful rest day behind us, we geared up for the 83 mile no-service stretch to Baker, the longest such stretch of the trip. We filled our bellies with Subway and stocked up heavily with water and food. Feeling well prepared, we set out into the desert around 9, later than our compatriots, but fairly early for us. Shortly outside of town, we met a father-son pair of cyclists coming the opposite direction. The greeted us with great enthusiasm and we exchanged tips for the ride ahead. After taking pictures with us, they departed, leaving us to reminisce about the heady early days of our own ride and return to the task of finishing it up. Our maps had informed us that the Great Basin region, which covers most of Nevada, consists of many small mountain ranges separated by about 20 miles of flat desert. We found exactly that on our route, repeatedly climbing 1-2 thousand feet before descending back onto plains. Descending the first such mountain, we encountered our second pronghorn antelope of the trip. Matt redeemed himself by not scaring this one, and the pronghorn entranced us by racing alongside our bikes through the sagebrush. Long after it broke away from the road, we were still able to see it, bounding all alone across the vast, empty landscape. Channeling our own inner pronghorns, we sped efficiently through the plains and powered over mountains, stopping only a couple of times for food breaks. It was not especially hot or sunny, so our only impediments were the occasional burst of rain(!) or headwinds. Coming down the last mountain before Baker, we began surging to over 20 mph, pushing Charlie to his limit. We finally stopped at the Utah-Nevada border for pictures, before cruising the last 7 miles into town, done with riding mid-afternoon for once. We met Chris, Matt, and Mercedes in one of the town’s two shops, where they were taking advantage of some complementary drinks. We settled down at a table and recounted our ride to them, at which point they exclaimed indignantly that the father-son cyclists had apparently not deemed them picture worthy. We had a good laugh and recovered from the ride with some thick slices of pizza. Mercedes was prepared and had found us a campsite at the local school. After dinner, we pitched our tents in the schoolyard and relaxed. A brief windstorm almost blew our tents away, but after that disturbance the night was peaceful.

After the late night we slept in until around ten. For good measure, we spent two hours getting ready and stocking up on food at the Family Dollar (they have super cheap dried mangos), meaning we didn’t get on the road until noon. Fortunately, the ride to Eureka was a relatively modest 78 miles, without any particularly large mountains. Shortly outside of Ely, the loneliest road in America lost any traces of human habitation, and our only company was once again the traffic that passed by every few minutes. Our passage across the low mountains and the endless valleys between was fairly smooth at first, but at a food stop in one of the valleys we saw a rainstorm in the distance that appeared to be approaching. Within minutes it was upon us, with hail and ferocious winds. We tucked our heads and once agin let our workhorse Peter plow through the wind. Though our progress was slowed, the wind eventually lessened and we resumed a brisk pace through the open, empty landscape. About 60 miles down the road, biking through a sagebrush covered valley that looked dishearteningly similar to what we’d been biking through all day, we were hit with yet more freak Nevada rain. Fortunately, this rain was accompanied by minimal wind, and we were able to enjoy the beauty if the shimmering water falling over the infinite plain. Unfortunately, Charlie spent too long filming the natural beauty, and the GoPro ran out of memory, with few prospects for buying a new memory card any time soon. Shortly after emerging from the second rainstorm, we encountered Chris and Matt once again. We rode up behind them as fast as we could, pretending to be cycling pirates of the high highways, but they were in intimidated. We slowed down from our pirate pace, and cruised with our Californian friends the remaining distance towards Eureka. Once in town, we stopped at one of the local bar/restaurant/casinos for food. Peter had the misfortune of ordering the $14 Caesar salad, with consisted of a chicken nugget sized chicken strip, a tomato, and iceberg lettuce with no dressing. As we ate, we talked to Chris, Matt, and Mercedes (who had gotten in much earlier), and decided that it was a good night to try camping out in the desert. Out plans were put on hold by a stop at a gas station to supplement Peter’s subpar dinner. By the time we left, it was getting dark, so we rode with urgency out of town looking for a good stretch of desert to camp in. Finding much of the desert fenced off, we settled on an arbitrary patch of sagebrush a short way from the road about a mile away from the nearest building. Fumbling in the dark, we walked until we felt far enough from the road and began pitching our tents. The cleared ground around us looked suspiciously like it was covered in giant anthills, but we trusted our tents to protect us. Camp setup went without incident for several minutes until California Matt shouted that he saw a rattlesnake. As Matt moved quickly away, we shined our meager lights and confirmed the presence of a fat rattlesnake right where Matt had been. Suddenly worried about snakes under every bush, we proceeded cautiously before sighting another rattlesnake just minutes later. Now we were really freaked out about being bitten, and we practically dove into our tents, hoping we would not have to get up in the night to pee. We talked nervously for a while, and those with iphones were miraculously able to get signal and look up rattlesnake bite first aid on wikipedia. Though jumpy, we began to drift to sleep under another starry desert night sky. Our uneasy sleep was not helped by the periodic howling of coyotes, which always seemed just a bit closer than they had been before, but we made it through the night in one piece.

After our tense night, we rose soon after the sun came up. Our patch of desert 30 feet from the road seemed much less sinister by the light of day, and we enjoyed a light-hearted morning with Chris, Matt and Mercedes joking about the previous night. We planned on cranking out 120+ miles to get to Carson City in 2 days, so we left our companions behind to bike at a faster pace. Of course, 10 miles into the ride Matt got a flat tire, so by the time he had replaced his inner tube, the others had caught up to us. Deciding we might as well ride together at this point, we regrouped and spent about 15 relaxed miles riding together. As we were biking, we saw a man with wild hair and a huge sack slung over his shoulder walking down the side of the road. California Matt stopped to check if he was ok and later related to us the fascinating story of Jeroulde. Jeroulde didn’t talk and communicated with facial expressions and by spelling out words on his hand. He had been walking around the country alone for around 15 years, apparently as some sort of spiritual journey. He had been as far south as Mexico, but thought it was too hot. California Matt offered him food, which he accepted without enthusiasm, and water which he drank eagerly. We were awed by Jeroulde’s odyssey, but we still had mileage to do, and soon we lost sight of him behind us, still walking alone across the vast, empty desert. After a leisurely brunch break at a pavilion in the middle of nowhere (aka Nevada), we decided we were a bit behind schedule, so we bid the Chris, Matt, and Mercedes goodbye and set out again with renewed vigor. Our determined pace faced a stiff test in the ~2500 feet of climbing before Austin, and we rolled into the hillside town hot and weary. We took the natural course of action and recuperated at a gas station with a half gallon of ice cream and some snacks. Throughout our meal, several people and a dog came by to investigate the sweaty strangers devouring a tub of ice cream, and we told them (except the dog) of our trip. With great reluctance, we left the gas station around an hour later and resumed our push to get halfway to Carson City. We passed the next 50 miles in a tired daze, with Peter pulling through the headwind and all of us peddling wordlessly, while the dance hits of Cascada urged us on. After an eternity of biking we saw the restaurant and rental cabins that make up Cold Springs Station. Though we had intended to get in 13 more miles to Middlegate, some maternal women at the bar convinced us that it was a shady alternative (Chris and Matt later stopped in Middlegate and said it was awesome). Grateful for an excuse to stop, we rented a cabin for the night for $40, and ordered food. We dig into a massive dinner (Matt and Peter had giant burgers and Charlie ate a whole pizza) under the eerie gaze of the numerous deer, elk, and pronghorn heads mounted on the wall. after dinner, Matt gave us a needed laugh by trying to bike through loose gravel and doing a slow motion sideways topple, with feet still clipped into pedals. We laughed at Matt, started our laundry, showered, and headed to bed exhausted.

With an 125 mile day planned, we attempted to wake up early, but didn’t actually hit the road until 9. The ride took us first towards Fallon, which was blessedly downhill. The road passed through the usual desolate desert and headwinds, but we found the desert changing from the bush-covered scrubland we were used to a Sahara-like ocean of sand. We passed some cryptically labeled military facilities like “Centroid testing facility” (we speculated that a centroid was some kind of top secret fighting robot). There were even some helicopters and jets visible on their flights around the base, though we didn’t see any of the B17 stealth bombers we, which had heard also use the base. Leaving behind the military areas, we passed through more desert notable for the nearby “sand mountain” (giant sand dune) and the countless messages spelled out on the sand with rocks by passersby. Biking through this interesting landscape, we met one of our eastbound counterparts, Dietmar. Dietmar was a proud naturalized American, and spoke enthusiastically about biking across the country. We agreed with his sentiment, but were grateful he was the one with 7 1/2 states to go and not us. A bit further down the road, we saw a lone figure walking on the side with what looked like a baby stroller. We had heard of some dude walking across the country, and we pulled over to meet the man behind the stories. Jonah (blog: dudetrek) was very friendly and repeatedly complemented us for our long bike trip, despite have walked the same distance and then some. Though his beard and demeanor made him seem older, he was younger than us at 19, and was taking a year off after high school for self discovery. He told us some of his stories, like the time he befriended some rafting guides in Moab and spent their day off rafting and partying with them. He even played us some Bob Marley on the guitar he carried. We spent 20 fascinating minutes talking to him, and came away impressed with how down-to-earth and normal he seemed for a guy WALKING from Delaware to California (for the record, he was 11 months in when we met him and he still had a month to go). After these interludes and a substantial amount of additional biking, we hit the tantalizing outskirts and finally the city of Fallon (a teeming metropolis of 8000). We were again ravenous, and we annihilated a small mountain of Chinese food, with Peter declaring it was the most he’d ever eaten at once. With little time to waste, we returned to the road around 4, hoping to cover the 60 miles to Carson City before dark. We could’ve probably done it too, but we decided to stop at WalMart for a new GoPro memory card, and then Matt got another flat tire. We finally left again around 5, now needing some major speed to hit Carson City before dark. Alas, the remaining miles did not cooperate, and we faced strong headwinds almost immediately. Charlie and then Matt pulled, with everyone pushing their tired legs to try and make the most of the remaining daylight. At a snack stop in Silver Springs, a family congratulated us on our trip, while an older man told us we “had a hell of a long way to San Francisco.” Resuming the ride, we began to run out of daylight still 15 miles from Carson City. We slowed down and turned on our bike lights, but still hit some bumps going over trash on the side of the road. Still, after an hour of tired but determined riding, we reached the promised land of Carson City. Like mana from heaven, we were greeted almost immediately by the first Dairy Queen in over a thousand miles, and we wasted not time in ordering large blizzards and food. Feeling slightly rejuvenated, we spent about 30 minutes searching before finally finding an America’s Best Value Inn to stop at. We hit a stroke of luck when the hotel clerk, Chris, heard we were going to Lake Tahoe in the morning a offered to give us a ride since he was also headed that way. Tired, but excited to have made it and to have a rest day, we fell fast asleep around 11:30.

As promised, Chris met us early in the morning to drive us to Lake Tahoe. We were still tired from the previous two days, but getting up early was much less daunting without any biking planned for the day. Chris spent the drive talking to us about his time living in California, and his subsequent move to Carson City. We were surprised that anyone would willingly move from California to Nevada, but he assured us that there was nowhere else with such great skiing at such an affordable cost of living. Chris dropped us off at Zephyr Cove on the shore of Lake Tahoe, which we had previously determined was the cheapest place for watersport rentals. We stopped by the dock to make our parasailing reservations, and then returned to the Zephyr Cove restaurant to eat and pass the time until our appointment. The Zephyr Cove restaurant had delicious but significantly overpriced omelettes, and Matt decided to get his money’s worth by gorging on the complementary jam packets at our table. After 7 packets, he recovered only a little value, but at least he had his dignity. We finally left for parasailing around 11, along with a grandmother, mother, and granddaughter group. Charlie and Peter went first, and enjoyed a fun liftoff followed by a serene 5 minutes flying high above the brilliant blue lake and forested mountains. Not content to let us enjoy the flight dry, the captain dipped our “feet” (or lower halves of our bodies) in the freezing water before reeling us back in. Matt and Peter went for another ride and had a similar experience, but with an even bigger dunk in the lake. After parasailing, we passed time by eating overpriced french fries and playing beach volleyball with some locals while we waited for things to warm up for jet skiing. After the local high school kids exposed our complete loss of hand-eye coordination in volleyball, we decided it was time for jet skiing, but were disappointed to discover rentals had been cancelled for the day due to high winds. Content with our experience of Tahoe, we embarked to catch the bus back to Carson City. We had some difficulty finding the bus stop, and arrived just as our bus was pulling away. Fortunately there were two more buses for the day. We mistakenly believed that sitting at the bus stop would cause the bus driver to stop to pick us up, but an hour later we were traumatized by the long-awaited bus blowing straight by our stop. Finally, two hours after we decided to leave, we managed to catch the last bus with frantic waving and jumping around. The bus ride itself was a small adventure, featuring a homeless man who argued constantly with the driver about his rights to have more than one seat, and a boy in high school who seemed in awe of our trip so far. Returning to Carson City, we had a relaxing dinner at yet another Chinese restaurant (Charlie opted for Indian food), and returned to the motel more than ready to take on the Sierra Nevadas.

After our hard-earned rest day, we were energized and excited to push through our final biking hurdle, the Sierra Nevada. We woke up relatively late, and after stocking up at the Family Dollar we set out along the scenic eastern side of the mountains. The desert finally began to recede and we saw farms and even sparse forests on the mountainside. The route also became steadily more uphill, and after a series of ever steeper hills we, took a pitstop at the last shop before the mountain climb began in earnest. At the store we celebrated our impending conquest with delicious cream soda and chips. As we left, a woman in the parking lot asked if we could we could give her directions, and ingratiated herself by lecturing us about how much she hated cyclists while we looked through our maps (while still on our bikes). After this prelude, we began the big climb over the last mountains between us and the Pacific. We had been warned by cyclists traveling from San Francisco of how challenging the Sierra Nevada was, but we had the advantages of starting at almost 5,000 feet in elevation and having 7 weeks of biking under our belts. The mountain turned out to be no match for our veteran legs and we raced through forests and alpine scenery towards Carson Pass. Peter was carrying the GoPro for once, so Charlie was able to assume his natural role leading the charge. After a brief celebration at the pass, we raced the daylight down the mountain towards the campsites near Kirkwood. We pulled over at a restaurant on the side of the road, thinking we saw our loyal friends Chris, Matt, and Mercedes waving at us. Upon stopping we realized the people waving at us were in fact a group of middle-aged women drinking. We struck up a conversation with the group (whose names I unfortunately forget, but included another Charlie and “JP girl,” named for her Japanese heritage), and told them about our trip. We mentioned how hungry we always were, and the women revealed that they were actually lunch ladies from a California school district vacationing together in a nearby cabin. Perhaps due to the beers or their lunch lady instincts, they graciously invited us back to their cabin to make camp and receive free food. We convinced them to also invite Chris and Matt when they turned up, even though “those guys don’t look as nice as you boys.” Thrilled at our luck, we arrived at their cabin and were showered in homemade cookies and juicy quesadillas. After stuffing ourselves, we all gathered around a campfire and made smores (with Reese’s cups instead of chocolate!) and talked into the night. Amidst brilliant stars, lush California trees and good company, we felt utterly relaxed.

After our memorable night high in the mountains, we awoke energized for our 110 mile descent towards Sacramento. Matt and Chris had left before we were up, but we thanked the lunch ladies and said our farewells before setting off. We had convinced ourselves that the ride would be one giant downhill through beautiful California scenery, but we were only half right, as the first few miles of the day were partially up some decent mountain ascents. Nonetheless, the vistas across the forested and snow-capped mountains were awe-inspiring. We biked by crystal blue lakes, and as we finally began to descend in altitude, we began to see green groves of especially large and impressive fir trees. Despite the exquisite natural beauty, the shoddy state of the backroads began to wear on our enjoyment of the ride. We were forced to ride our breaks and weave between potholes through much of the fir forest. Charlie even hit a pothole (as anyone could have done!) and had to bike back a quarter mile to collect the belongings that had been knocked off the back of the bike. The descent eventually leveled out somewhat, and we emerged into an area that seemed to be a combination of Napa Valley and Appalachia. There were vineyards and wineries all around, but also plenty of pickup trucks and people who talked like they were from rural PA. One man rolled into a parking lot we were resting by, loudly arguing about his recent purchase of a log-splitter with his girlfriend. The girlfriend stalked off to the bathroom, leaving the talkative guy to loudly attempt to charm us into buying the logsplitter hitched to his truck. Despite his loud optimism, we determined that we would not be able to bike effectively pulling a 500 lb log splitter. The day began to drag on as the wilderness thinned out and we hit increasingly heavy traffic approaching Folsom. Our mood was further dampened when a car stopped to ask us to call 911 for a car accident just a mile behind us (apparently a drunk driver). After alerting the police, we continued on extra wary of the many cars packed onto the roads alongside us. The day reached its low point not long after the 911 incident as we failed to find the obscure entrance to the Folsom bike path we were supposed to take. After 20 frustrating minutes of circling, we finally found the entrance hidden by bushes in the back of a random gravel lot. Fortunately, the search proved worthwhile, as the bike path was well maintained and the sun glinting off the river and through the path-side grass gave everything a beautiful golden tint. With daylight running low, we needed a push to cover the remaining 20 miles to Sacramento. We were starting to feel the day’s 100 miles, but our motivation was jump-started when another cyclist passed us for the first time in the entire trip! Despite our long day and heavy bags, we took to the challenge, pedaling close to 25 mph for several minutes to run our rival down. We had nearly caught him when the rogue cyclist abruptly turned off the bike path towards town, causing Matt to bitterly remark on the cyclist’s poor sportsmanship. With just a few miles left and barely any daylight, we continued to bike hard, crossing the bridge into Sacramento just as the sun set, before collapsing into bed at a conveniently placed Super 8.

We ate breakfast at the Super8 confident of reaching San Francisco within the day. Peter made extra certain of it by drinking 4 cups of coffee, a big step up from his usual 0. We then set out along the aptly named Yolo bike path (if you are too old for that reference), with only 60 miles between us and the Vallejo ferry. The bike path dropped us off in Davis, which reminded us of long-lost State College. Near Davis, we ran into Matt and Chris once again, and enjoyed a relaxing ride with them through the fruit farms of the Central Valley. After a traditional bike trip lunch at Subway, we arbitrarily decided to try to make it to Vallejo in time for the 2:00 ferry. Though there were later ferries that would not require as much of a rush, we figured it would be fitting end to the trip to give ourselves a bit of a challenge. We made plans with Chris and Matt to meet in San Francisco, and hit the road to Vallejo with new energy. The first 20 or so miles went according to plan, but we began to hit some rolling hills outside of Vallejo that put our 2:00 arrival in jeopardy. As we ascended a surprisingly intimidating hill overlooking the city, we realized we had less than 15 minutes to cover 5 miles through hills and stoplights to reach the ferry in time. Fortunately, caffeine-invigorated Peter took command and set a breakneck pace through Vallejo. Matt and Charlie later agreed that they though their hearts might explode, but with some fortunate green lights and 50 days of training under our belts, we made it to the ferry with 2 minutes to spare. After a well earned rest on the ferry we were dropped off in San Francisco. After spending 50 days almost entirely in rural backcountry and the outskirts of cities, we were overwhelmed by the bustling Embarcadero. There were techie looking people wearing Google Glass, sharply dressed professionals, and way more girls our age than we were used to. We soaked in the impending accomplishment of our goal before pulling into the disconcertingly well-designed bike lane to head to the Pacific Ocean. City biking took a bit of an adjustment, and more than once we had locals fly by us while we stopped at stop signs or waited for idled Pedicabs to move. After several fits and starts, and getting directions from some friendly old men sitting near the Golden Gate Bridge, we finally found our way to Baker Beach on the Pacific Ocean! We celebrated by taking pictures, before enduring a chilly wait for Matt and Chris to show up with some friends they were meeting. Once the others arrived, we reveled together in our feat, and of course took more pictures. After we got too cold for that, we left the beach and biked our last few miles to a hostel. Matt and Chris gave us a fascinating tour of the city along the the way before leaving us at a fancy Chinese restaurant. Though pricy, the meal felt thoroughly earned, and we ended the day happy and excited for our remaining adventures in California.

With our long journey finally over, we capped off the trip with a weeklong California vacation. Charlie’s sister’s roommate Jenna and her family were kind enough to host us in their home in Mill Valley, just across the Golden Gate Bridge. Jenna showed us the area’s excellent vegetarian food and beautiful overlooks, as well as treating our weary legs to sweet, sweet car rides. After our time with Jenna, we met up with our nerd friend Doug, who was in Palo Alto for an internship. At Doug’s we spent an embarrassing amount of time lounging around and enjoying being lazy. In between chill-out sessions, we found time to tour San Francisco, where we tackled a Vermonster (30 scoops of Ben and Jerry’s ice cream plus way too many toppings), mostly thanks to Matt. We also travelled to Yosemite National Park with Doug and soaked in the spectacular vistas and top-notch Facebook profile picture opportunities. And in a final adventure that seemed vaguely relevant to being in California, we went skydiving. Looking back, it seems surreal that we biked 3800 miles, from struggling to put up tents in the dark our first day in coastal Virginia, to arriving in San Francisco 50 later, sorer, and beardier. It was hard, but it was also deeply rewarding. We will always remember the hippy commune in Virginia, Pastor Joe’s hospitality and conspiracy theories, and our bonfire with the California lunch ladies. We bonded through shared hardship and triumph, and broke up our (relatively) normal lives with a big, fat adventure. Thank you to Dr. Page/Dad, the Saackes, the Bergers, the Danskies, the Tuttles, the Doug, and everyone else who helped us along the way!
